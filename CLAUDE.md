# Claude Code Context

## Design Context

### Users
General public exploring Philadelphia neighbourhoods — casual browsers curious about local character and how it changes over time. Secondary audience: technical interviewers assessing full-stack craft and product judgment. Users arrive with a question ("what's my neighbourhood like?") or pure curiosity, not a research agenda. They should orient immediately and be rewarded for deeper exploration.

### Brand Personality
**Curious · Precise · Alive**

Minimal and sharp enough to feel authoritative, but warm and playful enough to invite discovery. Not a dashboard — closer to an editorial data essay that moves. The data is rich; the aesthetic should honour it without overwhelming the viewer.

### Aesthetic Direction
**References:** The Pudding / NYT Graphics (editorial polish, storytelling with data) and Spotify Wrapped / Linear (bold color confidence, opinionated dark interfaces, satisfying micro-interactions).

**Anti-references:** Generic BI dashboard aesthetic, cluttered GIS tool, corporate "smart city" look.

**Theme:** Dark-first. Navy `#1a1a2e` base. The six Wong-adapted vibe colors are the hero — UI chrome should be minimal and recede. The map is the centrepiece.

**Emotional goal:** Aesthetic pleasure first. Beautiful to look at passively. Discovery and curiosity reward deeper engagement.

### Design Principles

1. **Beauty earns attention.** Aesthetic pleasure is the primary emotional goal. Every spacing decision, animation timing, and typographic choice should be deliberate.

2. **Chrome recedes, data shines.** The six vibe colors and the map are the heroes. Panel backgrounds and controls should be dark and unobtrusive.

3. **Precision over decoration.** No gratuitous flourishes. Animations serve meaning. Typography is functional but considered.

4. **Warmth in the details.** Micro-copy, hover states, and transitions should feel human — personality and wit without breaking the precision.

5. **Ship-quality polish.** No rough edges. Loading, empty, and error states are all polished. The standard: a senior engineer assumes it's a real product.
